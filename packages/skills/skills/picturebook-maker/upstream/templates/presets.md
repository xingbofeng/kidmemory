# Picturebook Maker Presets

## Story Voice Presets

### child-fable-reversal

Use when the story should feel like a child's question aimed at the adult world.

Distillation:
- Start from a thing a child can touch: egg, bed, schoolbag, door, shadow, clock.
- Let objects have opinions.
- Use short sentences and direct reversals.
- Ask why the world rule is so strange.
- End with curiosity, choice, dignity, or boundary.

Good for:
myths, social fables, education topics, moral choice stories, absurd everyday stories.

Avoid:
direct imitation of a named writer, preachy adult morals, dense explanations.

### tender-growth-adventure

Use when the story should feel warm, windy, alive, and quietly brave.

Distillation:
- The child or protagonist grows by doing, not by being lectured.
- Nature is not scenery; it reacts, hides, gives, refuses, heals.
- Conflict is rarely a pure villain. It is imbalance, fear, greed, forgetting, fatigue, or a broken promise.
- Use ordinary work: cooking, sweeping, carrying, repairing, planting, walking.
- Put wonder beside daily life.
- Let courage be small and repeated.
- End with restoration, responsibility, and a little open sky.

Good for:
growth stories, ecology, family, friendship, journey, repair, goodbye, courage.

Avoid:
"in the style of Hayao Miyazaki" prompts, copied creature designs, flying machines or spirits that echo specific films too closely.

Prompt language:
"tender growth adventure, hand-drawn warmth, everyday task becoming wonder, nature as a living presence, soft ambiguity, child-scale courage"

### curiosity-experiment

Use when the story should teach understanding through observation and play.

Distillation:
- Start by separating the name of a thing from how it works.
- Let the child test, poke, count, listen, draw, compare, or build a tiny model.
- Explain complex ideas through one concrete experiment.
- Admit what the character does not know.
- Replace "because adults say so" with "let's see what happens".
- End with a better question, not just a correct answer.

Good for:
science stories, math intuition, tools, machines, nature observation, learning habits.

Avoid:
lecture voice, textbook summary, fake experiments, jargon without demonstration.

Prompt language:
"curiosity-led story, observable mechanism, child experiment, names are not understanding, concrete demonstration, playful doubt"

### children-fable-collage

Reference lineage:
classic children's fable collage and torn-paper storytelling.

Visual rules:
- torn paper, visible fibers, matte texture
- simple shapes, symbolic animals or giants
- generous negative space
- soft shadows from layered paper
- low-saturation blues, ochre, moss green, clay brown
- one emotional image per page

Avoid:
named artist imitation, copied character shapes, glossy 3D, complex realism.

### naturalist-watercolor-miniature

Reference lineage:
British naturalist picture-book watercolor.

Visual rules:
- small animals, plants, cottages, gardens, field details
- delicate watercolor, fine ink, botanical observation
- warm restraint, careful scale, quiet humor
- backgrounds feel observed from life

Good for:
animal tales, seasonal stories, small domestic adventures.

Avoid:
copying specific rabbit/clothing designs or named book layouts.

### wild-crosshatched-dream

Reference lineage:
expressive crosshatched dream-world picture books.

Visual rules:
- pen-and-ink texture, dense hatching, dramatic silhouettes
- wild rooms, forests, moonlit islands, emotional monsters
- child psychology made visible
- warm but slightly unruly energy

Good for:
fear, anger, bedtime, imagination, inner monsters.

Avoid:
copying iconic monsters, bedroom layouts, or famous page compositions.

### bright-painted-paper-collage

Reference lineage:
painted-paper collage for very young children.

Visual rules:
- bold shapes, saturated color, clear silhouettes
- painted paper textures, cut edges, simple animals
- strong contrast and immediate readability
- page works even at thumbnail size

Good for:
ages 2-6, counting, food, animals, transformation, simple lifecycle stories.

Avoid:
copying iconic caterpillar, bear, or specific animal designs.

### nordic-ink-poetic-minimal

Reference lineage:
Nordic ink illustration and poetic children's fantasy.

Visual rules:
- sparse line, muted watercolor, white space
- gentle melancholy, sea wind, islands, cabins, forests
- small figures in large landscapes
- emotions are quiet, not over-explained

Good for:
loneliness, friendship, journey, home, seasons, inner growth.

Avoid:
copying specific troll-like characters or recognizable fictional worlds.

### loose-ink-watercolor-humor

Reference lineage:
loose comic ink and watercolor children's illustration.

Visual rules:
- energetic scratchy line
- imperfect body proportions
- expressive gestures, comic timing
- sparse backgrounds, fast readable action
- watercolor washes that do not over-polish

Good for:
funny school stories, mischievous children, dialogue-heavy scenes.

Avoid:
imitating a living illustrator's exact line, character faces, or book franchise.

### folk-papercut-myth

Reference lineage:
Chinese folk paper-cut, shadow puppet, and festival print language.

Visual rules:
- symmetrical or stage-like compositions
- red, black, gold, indigo, jade green
- cut-paper silhouettes, cloud motifs, waves, mountains, auspicious patterns
- theatrical mythic staging

Good for:
Chinese myths, festivals, origin stories, zodiac, heroic fables.

Avoid:
overcrowded ornament, unreadable tiny patterns, generic tourist-Chinese decoration.

### woodblock-folktale-flat

Reference lineage:
woodblock print and folk tale poster language.

Visual rules:
- flat color planes, carved line texture
- strong horizon and simple composition
- patterned water, clouds, trees, robes
- limited palette, print-like grain

Good for:
journey myths, sea stories, old villages, fables with moral clarity.

Avoid:
copying specific historical prints or famous compositions.
